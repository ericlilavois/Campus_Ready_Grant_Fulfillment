Executive Summary

Campus Ready Foundation provides one-time grants averaging $2,500 to cover the high upfront costs of moving from home to campus: travel, dorm setup, and early-arrival meals. Traditional financial aid rarely covers these expenses, forcing low-income families to choose between high-interest debt or requiring students to work excessive hours during their critical first semester.

Our support removes this immediate barrier so students arrive prepared and can engage early with confidence.

The Challenge

Move-in and initial living costs average $2,500 per student. These costs fall into a blind spot for most aid packages. Families in the $40,000 to $75,000 income range face a particular vulnerability. The High Cost of Delayed Graduation

Students who take longer than four years to graduate face substantial financial penalties that extend throughout their careers.

The Delay Penalty

Taking an extra year to graduate costs $85,000 to $90,000 in lost lifetime earnings. Two extra years costs approximately $174,000. Students who graduate in five years "break even" on college costs by age 37, compared to age 34 for four-year graduates.

The mechanism is straightforward: delayed graduates miss a year of professional-level earnings and enter the workforce behind their peers. This creates a wage gap that persists throughout their careers.

  • A four-year graduate earns approximately $37,500 in their first year post-graduation
  • By age 25 (three years of experience), they earn around $45,500
  • A five-year graduate at age 25 has only two years of experience and earns $43,000
  • This $2,500 annual gap compounds over 40+ years of working life

Additionally, delayed graduation is associated with 8-15% lower post-college earnings, depending on the length of delay, even after controlling for other factors.

Sources: Federal Reserve Bank of New York (2020, 2023); Research in Higher Education (Delayed Time-to-Degree study, 2019)

Intergenerational Effects: The Sibling Advantage

When a student successfully completes college, the benefits extend beyond that individual to influence younger siblings and family economic trajectory.

The Sibling Ripple Effect

Research shows that 19-20% of younger siblings enroll at the same college as their older sibling, and 69% enroll in the same type of institution (four-year vs. two-year). When an older sibling attends college, younger siblings are 15-20 percentage points more likely to attend a four-year college themselves.

Older siblings serve as crucial information sources about the college experience, transmitting knowledge about the application process, financial aid, campus life, and career returns. This effect is particularly strong in families where parents lack college experience.

  • When an older sibling enrolls at a high-quality college, it nearly quadruples the probability that a younger sibling applies to the same institution (from 10% to 37%)
  • Younger siblings double their probability of applying only to colleges with graduation rates above 50% (from 25% to 53%)
  • The effect is strongest for disadvantaged families, where parents cannot provide the same information
  • One student's success creates a pathway for siblings, amplifying the impact of each Campus Ready investment

Sources: Harvard Kennedy School research (Goodman, Hurwitz, Smith, et al., 2015-2019); Sociology journals on sibling effects in education

How We Select & Safeguard

Eligibility & Documentation (must have)

  • Who we support: First-time college students attending 4-year residential programs or coommunity colleges with dorms.
  • Housing verification (required / pass-fail): Provide one of: housing contract/assignment, acceptance letter that mentions housing, housing deposit receipt, or official housing application approval. Applications that fail this step are not considered, regardless of score.
  • Enrollment confirmation: Acceptance letter or student-portal screenshot showing intent to enroll or enrollment status.

Objective & Subjective Scoring (fairness and consistency)

  • Ties: Prioritize first-gen; Pell-eligible (or comparable indicator); verified homelessness/foster-care/special circumstances; if still tied, earlier application date.

Verification & Review Flow (controls)

  • Two-point verification: (1) Documents check (housing + enrollment), (2) data checks (income/household size, distance) via application inputs and system calculations.
  • Reviewer calibration: Board uses identical scoring guidelines; periodic calibration to align standards.

  • Travel: Virtual card issued per student for travel bookings. Where feasible, enable MCC (Merchant Category Code) / category locks and date windows so funds are usable only for eligible travel purchases in the move-in window.

  • Boundaries: No peer-to-peer (P2P) payment apps. Replacement cards allowed; no receipt-upload requirement and no random post-move-in audits.

Why This Moment Matters

The freshman year establishes patterns that persist throughout college. Students who begin in debt or working excessively face immediate disadvantages that compound over time:

  • First Semester: Adjusting to college while managing 20+ work hours and financial stress
  • First Year: Falling behind on credits, missing involvement opportunities, experiencing lower GPA
  • Sophomore Year: Further behind academically, weak faculty relationships, no internship experience
  • Junior Year: Cannot afford study abroad or unpaid internships, limited professional network
  • Senior Year: Graduates late (if at all) with a thin resume and weak professional connections

Campus Ready interrupts this cascade at the source. Students arrive on campus financially stable, able to focus on academics, and positioned to engage fully in their education.
